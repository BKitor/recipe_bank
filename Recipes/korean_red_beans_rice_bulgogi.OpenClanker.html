Korean Red Beans & Rice Bowl with Bulgogi Beef

Louisiana-style creamy red beans flavored with gochujang + doenjang, served over rice with bulgogi beef, spinach namul, and sautéed zucchini. Designed to be meal-prep friendly and assembled like a bibimbap bowl.

Yield: 4–6 servings
Total time: ~2 hr (less with soaked beans)
Difficulty: Moderate
Equipment: Large pot, skillet, rice cooker (optional)
Important: Use unsweetened red beans (dry or canned). Do not use sweetened “red bean paste” products.

Ingredients

Korean-Style Red Beans (the “stew”)

  • 1 lb (450 g) dry red kidney beans (or small red beans)
  • 2 tbsp neutral oil (canola, avocado, etc.)
  • 1 large yellow onion, diced
  • 4 scallions, sliced (separate whites/greens)
  • 8 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tbsp ginger, minced
  • 6–8 cups water or unsalted chicken stock
  • 2–4 tbsp gochujang (adjust to taste)
  • 1–2 tbsp doenjang
  • 2 tbsp soy sauce (plus more to finish)
  • 1 tbsp gochugaru (optional, for cleaner chili flavor)
  • 1 tsp brown sugar or honey (optional)
  • 1–2 tbsp rice vinegar (or kimchi juice)
  • 1–2 tsp toasted sesame oil (finish)
  • Black pepper, to taste

Optional additions (recommended): 8 oz mushrooms (shiitake/oyster), sliced. Add with the pastes.

Rice

  • 2 cups short-grain white rice (or medium grain)
  • Water per your rice cooker / package instructions
  • 1 tsp toasted sesame oil (optional)
  • 1 tbsp toasted sesame seeds (optional)

Bulgogi Beef

  • 1.25 lb (560 g) thin-sliced ribeye or sirloin (bulgogi cut)
  • 1 small Asian pear, grated (or 1 kiwi, or 1/2 apple)
  • 1/2 cup soy sauce
  • 2 tbsp brown sugar (or honey)
  • 2 tbsp mirin (optional but recommended)
  • 1 tbsp toasted sesame oil
  • 5 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tbsp ginger, minced
  • 1/2 tsp black pepper
  • 1 tbsp toasted sesame seeds
  • 1/2 onion, thinly sliced

Spinach Namul (Blanched Spinach Side)

  • 10 oz (280 g) spinach
  • 1 tsp kosher salt (for blanching water)
  • 1 tsp soy sauce
  • 1–2 tsp toasted sesame oil
  • 1 small garlic clove, minced or grated
  • 1 tbsp toasted sesame seeds

Sautéed Zucchini (Hobak Bokkeum)

  • 2 medium zucchini, thin-sliced into half moons
  • 1 tbsp neutral oil
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1/2 tsp kosher salt (adjust to taste)
  • 1 tsp toasted sesame oil (optional)
  • 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds (optional)
  • Pinch of gochugaru (optional)

Instructions

1) Prep the beans

  1. Pick through beans for stones/debris. Rinse thoroughly.
  2. Optional soak: cover with water and soak 8–12 hours. Drain. (Soaking shortens cook time and improves texture.)

2) Start the bean base

  1. Heat a large pot over medium heat. Add neutral oil.
  2. Add diced onion and scallion whites. Cook 6–8 minutes until softened.
  3. Add garlic and ginger. Cook 45–60 seconds until fragrant.
  4. Add beans and 6 cups water/stock. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a steady simmer.
  5. Simmer uncovered 45–60 minutes, stirring occasionally.
Why add gochujang/doenjang later? It keeps the fermented flavor brighter and reduces bitterness.

3) Add Korean seasoning

  1. Once beans are starting to soften (after ~45–60 minutes), stir in:
    • 2–4 tbsp gochujang
    • 1–2 tbsp doenjang
    • 2 tbsp soy sauce
    • Optional: gochugaru and sugar/honey
  2. Continue simmering another 45–90 minutes until beans are very tender.
  3. Add more water as needed to keep a thick-stew consistency.

4) Thicken like Louisiana red beans

  1. When beans are fully tender, mash ~20% of them against the side of the pot.
  2. Simmer 10 more minutes. The pot should become creamy and cohesive.
  3. Finish with rice vinegar (or kimchi juice), black pepper, and sesame oil.
  4. Taste and adjust salt with soy sauce.

Target consistency: thick stew that still pours. If it gets too thick, add water 1/4 cup at a time.

5) Cook the rice

  1. Rinse rice until water runs mostly clear (2–4 rinses).
  2. Cook using your rice cooker or stovetop method.
  3. Optional: fold in sesame oil + sesame seeds after cooking.

6) Bulgogi beef

  1. In a bowl, combine grated pear, soy sauce, sugar, mirin, sesame oil, garlic, ginger, pepper, sesame seeds.
  2. Add beef and sliced onion. Mix thoroughly.
  3. Marinate 30 minutes (or up to overnight).
  4. Heat a skillet or grill pan over high heat. Cook beef in batches 2–4 minutes, until browned and cooked through.
Batch cooking matters: overcrowding steams the meat and you lose the caramelized edges.

7) Spinach namul

  1. Bring a pot of water to a boil. Salt it lightly.
  2. Add spinach and blanch 30–60 seconds until wilted bright green.
  3. Drain and rinse under cold water. Squeeze out as much water as possible.
  4. Toss spinach with soy sauce, sesame oil, garlic, and sesame seeds.

8) Sautéed zucchini

  1. Heat a skillet over medium-high heat. Add neutral oil.
  2. Add zucchini and cook 3–5 minutes until softened but not mushy.
  3. Add garlic and salt. Cook 30–60 seconds.
  4. Optional: finish with sesame oil, sesame seeds, and a pinch of gochugaru.

Assembly (Bowl Format)

  1. Add rice to a bowl.
  2. Ladle Korean red beans over the rice.
  3. Top with bulgogi beef.
  4. Add spinach namul and sautéed zucchini on the side.
  5. Optional toppings: kimchi, sliced scallion greens, gim/nori strips, fried egg.

Timing Plan (Efficient Workflow)

  1. Start beans simmering.
  2. While beans cook, marinate bulgogi.
  3. Cook rice ~30–45 minutes before serving.
  4. Cook spinach + zucchini in the last 15 minutes.
  5. Cook bulgogi last (fast and best hot).

Storage Notes

  • Beans improve overnight. Refrigerate up to 4–5 days.
  • Bulgogi keeps 3–4 days refrigerated.
  • Spinach and zucchini keep 2–3 days; reheat gently.
  • Freeze beans up to 3 months (texture holds well).

Adjustments / Variations

  • More heat: add gochugaru or a spoon of chili crisp at serving.
  • More funk: increase doenjang slightly, or add kimchi juice at the end.
  • More body: mash more beans, or simmer uncovered longer.
  • Vegetarian version: use stock made from kombu + dried shiitake instead of meat.

This recipe is intended as a fusion bowl: the beans are cooked like Louisiana red beans, but seasoned like a Korean stew. Keep the toppings bright and fresh so the dish doesn’t feel heavy.
